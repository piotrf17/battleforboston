Cost

The Battle for Boston is intended to be a tournament accessible to college-aged individuals, and as such is run as a not-for-profit tournament by the NECKC/NAKF, non-profit organizations dedicated to teaching karate. Because of this, a primary concern of the tournament directors is that competitors are not prevented from competing due to cost. However, some entry fees are required in order to pay for EMTs, facilities rental, trophies, and other expenses.

Please see the NAKF website for the fee structure for the individual part of the tournament.

For the college team part of the tournament, we are charging a single $125 fee per team to simplify the cost structure. This is the amount that it would typically cost to pre-register five students for individual events.

The NAKF/NECKC does not want tournament costs to prevent individuals from competing. Please contact the tournament director if registration fees would prevent participation. However, due to the non-profit nature of the NAKF/NECKC, our ability to provide assistance is limited.

There is no cost for spectators to attend, so encourage your friends and family to come watch you compete.

Safety Equipment

For all sparring competitions, competitors are expected to dress in appropriate clothing (such as a Gi). All competitors are required to wear safety equipment including a groin protector, a chest protector for women, a mouthguard, shin/instep pads, and karate mitts. Karate mitts must be non-cloth (i.e. dipped foam).

Head protection is required for beginners, and may be required in other divisions. A limited number of headgear will be available for loan, but competitors should plan on providing their own.

Team Kumite

Team Kumite pits teams of 5 against each other in head to head combat. In each round, the members of the team fight in successive kumite bouts against the opposing team. Whichever team wins three or more of the bouts advances to the next round. As few as three members may form a team, but a lack of team members will result in forfeits for those matches, so all three members of the 3-man team must win their match in order to win.

Competitors must present a final fighting order to the head judge as well as the scoring table before the start of each round. No further changes to the fighting order are allowed until the start of the next round. Any changes to the team roster due to injury must be approved by the head judge and head table, and are only permitted between rounds.

Each regular kumite bout will last for two minutes. The result of each bout is determined by a contestant obtaining a clear lead of five points, or at time-up having the highest number of points. In the event of a tie, an extra deciding bout not exceeding one minute will be fought. If after the extra deciding bout there is still a tie, the decision will be made by a vote of the referee and judges.

Valid targets to aim for are the head, face, neck, abdomen, chest, back, and side. However, for safety reasons no contact is allowed to the head, face, or neck except in the advanced division where light contact to the head and face is allowed. In no situation is contact to the throat permitted, and this will result in disqualification. Any scoring technique will award the contestant 1 point.

To be scored, a technique must be made with good form, a sporting attitude, vigorous application, awareness, good timing, and correct distance. The tournament floor is not padded, so the throws which are allowed is limited for safety reasons.

There are also a variety of techniques or behavior which are prohibited for safety reasons or to encourage a good competitive environment and will result in a penalty. These include

  • Excessive contact or throat contact.
  • Attacks to arms, legs, groin, joints, or instep.
  • Attacks to the face with open hand techniques.
  • Dangerous or forbidden throwing techniques.
  • Feigning injury.
  • Repeatedly leaving the ring.
  • Self-endangerment.
  • Avoiding combat to prevent the opponent from having an opportunity to score.
  • Clinching, wrestling, pushing, seizing, or standing chest to chest without attempting a throw or other technique.
  • Uncontrollable attacks.
  • Attacks with the head, knees, or elbows.
  • Discourteous behavior towards the opponent or referee.
  • Attacks made outside of the ring, before the match is started, or after the referee ends the point.

Team Kata

Teams will consist of five members, but are coed and have no age brackets. Members of opposing teams will perform the first five Heian kata, one by one, side by side. Judges will decide which team performed the kata best, and a point will go to that team; whichever team wins three or more of the bouts advances to the next round. As few as three members may form a team, but a lack of team members will result in forfeits for those matches, so all three members of the 3-man team must win their match in order to win.

The primary judging criteria are independent of whether the competitor is doing Shotokan Karate, or is using a weapon. Kata is not a dance or theatrical performance. It must be realistic in fighting terms and display concentration, power, and potential impact in its techniques. It must demonstrate strength, power, and speed -- as well as grace , rhythm, and balance. Specific criteria include:

  • Realistic Interpretations of Kata meaning.
  • Timing, rhythm, speed, balance, and focus of power are appropriate.
  • Correct use of breathing.
  • Correct focus of attention and concentration.
  • Correct and stable stances.
  • Proper tension in the abdomen and smooth movement.
  • Kata appropriateness for the skill level of the competitor.
